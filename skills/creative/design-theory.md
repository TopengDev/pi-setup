# Design Theory Framework — Loaded During Creative Ideation

These principles MUST inform every design decision. When self-critiquing generated output, evaluate against these theories. When a design violates a principle, reject and iterate.

---

## TIER 1 — Visual Fundamentals
The grammar of design. Without these, everything else falls apart.

### 1. The Gestalt Principles
How the human brain groups and perceives visual elements:
- **Proximity** — things close together are seen as related
- **Similarity** — things that look alike are grouped mentally
- **Continuity** — the eye follows lines and curves naturally
- **Closure** — the brain fills in missing parts of incomplete shapes
- **Figure/Ground** — the relationship between a subject and its background
- **Common Fate** — elements moving in the same direction feel connected

This is why some logos feel "off" even when you can't explain why. Gestalt is the reason.

### 2. Visual Hierarchy
Controlling where the eye goes first, second, third. Achieve through size, weight, color, contrast, and position. Every great design has a clear reading order — the viewer should never feel lost.

### 3. Contrast
The most powerful tool in design. Not just light vs. dark — contrast of size, shape, texture, color temperature, weight, and style. No contrast = no interest.

### 4. Balance
Symmetrical (formal, stable, trustworthy) vs. asymmetrical (dynamic, modern, energetic). Neither is better — knowing when to use which is the skill.

### 5. Proportion & Scale
The relationship between sizes of elements. Deliberately making things unexpectedly large or small creates tension and interest.

### 6. White Space (Negative Space)
Empty space is not wasted space — it's an active design element. It creates breathing room, directs focus, and signals premium quality. Beginners fear it. Masters weaponize it.

### 7. Alignment
Nothing should be placed arbitrarily. Every element should have a visual connection to something else. Even one pixel of misalignment is felt subconsciously.

### 8. Repetition
Repeating visual elements (colors, fonts, shapes, spacing) creates cohesion and makes a design feel intentional and unified.

## TIER 2 — Color Theory
Color is emotion made visible.

### 9. The Color Wheel & Relationships
- **Complementary** — opposite colors, maximum contrast, high energy
- **Analogous** — neighboring colors, harmonious and natural
- **Triadic** — three evenly spaced colors, vibrant but balanced
- **Split-complementary** — softer than complementary, beginner-friendly
- **Monochromatic** — one hue in different tones, ultra clean

### 10. Hue, Saturation & Value (HSV)
Hue = the color itself. Saturation = how vivid or muted. Value = how light or dark. Mastering these three dimensions lets you create sophisticated, non-clashing palettes.

### 11. Color Temperature
Warm colors (red, orange, yellow) advance visually and feel energetic. Cool colors (blue, green, purple) recede and feel calm. Use to create depth without any 3D technique.

### 12. Color Psychology
- Red → urgency, passion, danger
- Blue → trust, calm, professionalism
- Yellow → optimism, attention, caution
- Green → growth, nature, health
- Black → luxury, power, sophistication
- Orange → energy, friendliness, creativity

Context is everything — red means luck in Chinese culture, mourning in South African culture. Great designers know their audience.

### 13. Color Contrast (Accessibility)
WCAG contrast ratios. Text must be readable against its background. Not just ethical — makes designs work harder.

## TIER 3 — Typography
The single skill that separates good designers from great ones.

### 14. Type Anatomy
Baseline, x-height, ascender, descender, serif, stem, bowl, counter. Knowing anatomy helps choose and pair typefaces intelligently.

### 15. Type Classification
- **Serif** — traditional, trustworthy, editorial (Times, Garamond, Georgia)
- **Sans-serif** — modern, clean, digital (Helvetica, Inter, Futura)
- **Script** — elegant, personal, decorative
- **Display/Decorative** — expressive, used sparingly for headlines
- **Monospace** — technical, code-like (Courier, JetBrains Mono)

### 16. Type Pairing
Pair a serif with a sans-serif. Maximum 2–3 typefaces per project. Contrast in personality, harmony in weight.

### 17. Typographic Scale & Rhythm
Use a modular scale (like golden ratio: 1.618×) to create harmonious size progression across H1, H2, body, caption.

### 18. Kerning, Tracking & Leading
- **Kerning** — spacing between specific letter pairs
- **Tracking** — overall letter spacing across a word or block
- **Leading** — line height (too tight = suffocating, too loose = disconnected)

Bad tracking is the fastest way to make a design look amateur.

### 19. Hierarchy in Typography
Bold headline. Medium subheading. Regular body. Light caption. The typographic stack should guide the eye without the reader thinking about it.

## TIER 4 — Layout & Composition
How you arrange everything on the canvas.

### 20. The Grid System
Invisible structure that makes layouts feel organized. Column grids, modular grids, baseline grids. Everything snaps to a system.

### 21. The Rule of Thirds
Divide canvas into 3×3 grid. Place focal elements at intersections. Creates natural, balanced compositions.

### 22. The Golden Ratio (φ = 1.618)
Nature's proportion. When elements relate by 1.618, compositions feel inherently satisfying.

### 23. The Z-Pattern & F-Pattern
- **Z-pattern** for low-text designs (ads, posters)
- **F-pattern** for text-heavy layouts (articles, websites)

Place most important elements along the scan path.

### 24. Focal Point & Visual Flow
Every composition needs one dominant element. Then guide the eye through the rest in deliberate sequence — using size, color, lines, and contrast as arrows.

## TIER 5 — Advanced Conceptual Theories
Where technical skill becomes art.

### 25. Semiotics
The study of signs and symbols and how they carry meaning. Every shape, color, and form communicates something beyond its literal appearance.

### 26. Visual Metaphor
Using one image or form to represent an idea. The FedEx arrow. The Amazon smile (A to Z). The best logos contain a hidden metaphor.

### 27. Figure-Ground Manipulation
Advanced use of negative space to hide a second image (like the WWF panda, or FedEx arrow). One of the highest-level visual skills.

### 28. Design Psychology (Perception)
- **Doherty Threshold** — users engage more when response is under 400ms
- **Von Restorff Effect** — the thing that looks different gets remembered
- **Peak-End Rule** — people remember the most intense moment and the ending

### 29. Brand Identity Theory
Visual identity isn't just a logo — it's a system. Color palette, typography, spacing, tone, photography style, iconography. Consistency across touchpoints builds recognition.

### 30. Design Thinking
The process: Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test. Design is problem-solving. The most beautiful design that doesn't solve the right problem is decoration, not design.

---

## BONUS — Supporting Theories

### 31. The 60-30-10 Color Rule
60% dominant color, 30% secondary, 10% accent. A safe palette formula that always works.

### 32. Occam's Razor in Design
The simplest solution is usually the best. When in doubt, remove.

### 33. Hick's Law
More choices = longer decision time. Reduce options to increase clarity and action.

### 34. Fitts's Law
The larger and closer a target, the easier to interact with. Critical for UI/button design.

### 35. The CRAP Principles
Contrast, Repetition, Alignment, Proximity. Robin Williams' distillation of all fundamental design rules into four words.
